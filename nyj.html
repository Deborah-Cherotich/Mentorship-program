Born A Crime

Trevor Noah's "Born a Crime" delves into themes that hold relevance for current events in Trans-Nzoia County. The first chapter explores the impact of Apartheid, which leveraged linguistic and tribal differences to create division among communities, much like the Xhosa and Zulu. Noah's personal experience growing up in a mixed family, with a Xhosa mother and white Swiss father, offers a unique perspective on these issues.

The book highlights a variety of themes, one of which is religion. We learn that Noah's mother is a woman of faith who attends three different churches every Sunday, each offering something unique. While the first church focuses on praise, the second church delves deep into scripture analysis, which Trevor's mother particularly enjoys. This portrayal of religion reminds us of the diversity of beliefs and practices in different regions, such as Trans-Nzoia County, where people have their own unique beliefs and preferences when it comes to churches.

The second chapter of the book sheds light on the plight of indigenous people who were forced to leave their ancestral land and subjected to slavery. Trevor, born to a mixed-race couple, lived in constant fear of arrest due to discriminatory laws. However, his mother, a typist by profession, displayed remarkable courage and refused to cower in adversity. This narrative strikes a chord with our county, where poverty has forced people from diverse backgrounds to work as slaves in other communities.

A similar theme of belonging emerged in Trans-Nzoia County in 2007 when the Maasai and Luhya communities clashed due to differences in social status. The Maasai wanted the Luhya to vacate the land, leading to a violent confrontation. This scenario mirrors the historical conflict between the Zulu and Xhosa tribes, as depicted in "Born a Crime," where the Zulu warriors fought against the Xhosas, believing they held undue power over them.
